Molecular Orbital Diagram

Diagram Interpretation

The molecular orbital diagram shows the relative energy levels of atomic and molecular orbitals. Bonding orbitals (blue) are lower in energy than their parent atomic orbitals, while antibonding orbitals (red) are higher in energy.

Electrons are filled from the lowest energy orbitals following the Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund's rule. The bond order is calculated as (Bonding electrons - Antibonding electrons)/2.

Unpaired electrons (single red dots) indicate paramagnetic behavior, while all paired electrons (black and red pairs) indicate diamagnetic behavior.

Molecular Orbital Theory

Basic Principles

Molecular orbital (MO) theory describes the behavior of electrons in molecules in terms of combinations of atomic orbitals. When atoms bond to form molecules, their atomic orbitals combine to form molecular orbitals that are spread over the entire molecule.

Key Concepts

  • Bonding Orbitals: Lower in energy than the original atomic orbitals, promote bonding
  • Antibonding Orbitals: Higher in energy than the original atomic orbitals, denoted with a *
  • Sigma (σ) Bonds: Formed by head-on overlapping of orbitals
  • Pi (π) Bonds: Formed by side-by-side overlapping of p orbitals
  • Bond Order: (Number of bonding electrons - Number of antibonding electrons) / 2

Orbital Ordering

The relative energy ordering of molecular orbitals changes across the periodic table:

  • For Z < 8 (Before Oxygen): σ(2s) < σ*(2s) < π(2px) = π(2py) < σ(2pz) < π*(2px) = π*(2py) < σ*(2pz)
  • For Z ≥ 8 (Oxygen and beyond): σ(2s) < σ*(2s) < σ(2pz) < π(2px) = π(2py) < π*(2px) = π*(2py) < σ*(2pz)

Magnetic Properties

Molecules with unpaired electrons are paramagnetic (attracted to magnetic fields), while those with all electrons paired are diamagnetic (slightly repelled by magnetic fields).

Practical Applications

Molecular orbital theory helps explain:

  • Why O₂ is paramagnetic despite having an even number of electrons
  • The relative bond strengths in diatomic molecules
  • Electronic transitions in spectroscopy
  • Conductivity in metals and semiconductors
